# Harmonix Benchmark Methodology

## Abstract
This document outlines the academic foundation of the **Harmonix Benchmark Suite**. It demonstrates the superiority of the "Extreme Dependent Space" approach over traditional penalty-based optimization methods. By analytically embedding physical constraints directly into the search space rather than evaluating them within the objective function, Harmonix completely eliminates or drastically reduces the need for penalty functions. This approach ensures that the optimizer strictly explores physically feasible designs, resulting in faster and deeper convergence toward the global optimum.

## Mathematical Transformation: Penalty vs. Dependent Space

In classical constrained optimization, a problem is formulated as minimizing $f(x)$ subject to inequalities $g_i(x) \le 0$.
The traditional **Static Penalty** approach transforms this into an unconstrained problem via a penalty parameter $R$:
$$ \Phi(x) = f(x) + R \sum_{i} \max(0, g_i(x))^2 $$

The optimizer samples blindly from a rectangular hyperspace defined by flat upper and lower bounds. A vast majority of these samples are geometrically or physically impossible, wasting computational power.

In contrast, the **Extreme Dependent Space** approach relies on mathematical transformation and inversion of constraints. If a constraint links two variables (e.g., $D \le 1.5 - d$), we declare $d$ as the independent variable and embed the constraint into the upper bound of $D$:
$$ D_{upper} = \min(D_{max}, \, 1.5 - d) $$

By structuring variables hierarchically (e.g., $d \rightarrow D \rightarrow N$), complex constraints like volume, stress, and deflection become dynamic bounds for subsequent variables.

## The "Zero-Penalty" Concept

The pinnacle of this methodology is achieved in the `full_parametric_extreme` implementations. By utilizing bisection algorithms to find theoretical limits (e.g., the exact minimum radius $R_{min}$ required to satisfy a volume constraint at maximum cylinder length) and inverting rational equations, **all** geometric constraints are embedded into the design space.

The "Zero-Penalty" concept means:
- The design space generator *mathematically guarantees* that $g_i(x) \le 0$.
- The objective function becomes pure cost: $\Phi(x) = f(x) + 0.0$.
- The algorithm spends 100% of its computational effort exploiting the feasible region.

### 1. Constraint Inversion (Pressure Vessel, Spring, Speed Reducer)
Standard algebraic inversion of inequality constraints into boundary lambdas (e.g., $D \le 1.5 - d$ becomes `D_upper = min(D_max, 1.5 - d)`). Each constraint is removed from penalty evaluation entirely.

### 2. Hierarchical Chaining (Speed Reducer)
Circular constraint networks are resolved by structuring variables hierarchically ($m \rightarrow l_1 \rightarrow z \rightarrow d_1 \rightarrow d_2 \rightarrow b$). Each variable's bounds are computed from previously resolved variables, eliminating all 11 constraints from the penalty function.

### 3. Dynamic Feasibility Parsing (Retaining Wall Design)
The Retaining Wall benchmark expands the Zero-Penalty philosophy to combinatorial extraction. Instead of generating a discrete integer representing a standard ACI rebar combination and penalizing if it fails shear or spacing bounds limiters, the bounding `lambda` scans the entire 400+ unit catalog against the instantaneously generated foundation geometry. Only the structurally valid coordinates are preserved for the spatial mapper. This, combined with active bisection loops matching $FS_{overturning}$ guarantees absolutely zero physically impossible walls.
This dramatically improves the convergence rate (speed-up) and depth (solution quality) because the algorithm is no longer "pulled back" into the feasible zone by artificial penalties.

## Experimental Setup

To ensure fair comparison across all benchmark problems (Welded Beam, Pressure Vessel, Spring Design, Speed Reducer, Robot Gripper, and **Retaining Wall**), the **Harmony Search** algorithm parameters have been standardized based on numerical requirements.

- **Harmony Memory Size (HMS):** 50
- **Harmony Memory Considering Rate (HMCR):** Set between 0.90 and 0.95 depending on the density of the feasible region. Very high HMCR (0.95) is used for the Spring Design problem due to its incredibly tight metric scale, encouraging heavy exploitation of the memory.
- **Pitch Adjusting Rate (PAR):** Set between 0.35 and 0.40. A lower PAR prevents disruptive jumps in highly sensitive models.
- **Maximum Iterations:** 30,000 for continuous problems (Welded Beam, Pressure Vessel, Spring Design, Speed Reducer, Robot Gripper). **15,000** for the Retaining Wall problem, due to the substantially longer per-iteration evaluation time (~14.5s total vs ~1-2s for continuous problems).

### Reading the Visualizations
The plots generated by `ConvergencePlotter` are designed for academic interpretation:
- **Red lines** represent the Static Penalty baseline.
- **Green/Blue/Orange lines** represent dependent space iterations.
- **Absence of Penalty Spikes:** In dependent space configurations, the penalty graph line flatlines at 0.0 due to constraint embedding.
- **Descent Steepness:** The steepness of the green/blue/orange lines represents the rapid discovery of feasible low-cost designs, skipping the penalty "burn-in" phase present in the red line.
